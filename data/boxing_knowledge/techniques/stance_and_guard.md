# Stance and Guard

The stance and guard are the foundation of everything in boxing. Every punch, every defensive movement, and every step starts and ends in the stance. A solid stance provides balance, power generation, and defensive readiness.

## Orthodox Stance (Right-Handed)

### Foot Position
1. **Lead Foot**: Left foot forward, toes pointing toward the opponent at roughly a 45-degree angle.
2. **Rear Foot**: Right foot back, toes pointing to the right at roughly 45 degrees.
3. **Width**: Feet are shoulder-width apart. Too narrow and you lose balance; too wide and you lose mobility.
4. **Depth**: The rear foot is roughly one and a half shoulder-widths behind the lead foot.
5. **Weight Distribution**: Approximately 50/50 between both feet, or a slight 55/45 bias toward the rear foot for power generation.

### Body Position
- **Knees**: Slightly bent -- never locked. A slight bend allows explosive movement in any direction.
- **Hips**: Angled roughly 45 degrees from the opponent. This presents a smaller target.
- **Torso**: Upright but relaxed. Leaning too far forward exposes you; leaning back robs you of punching range.
- **Shoulders**: The lead shoulder is slightly higher to provide chin protection. Both shoulders stay relaxed.

### Guard Position
- **Lead Hand**: Held at cheekbone height, 6-8 inches in front of the face. Elbow points down and stays close to the ribcage.
- **Rear Hand**: Pressed against the right cheekbone, knuckles touching the jawline. This is the last line of defense.
- **Chin**: Tucked down behind the lead shoulder. Never let the chin float up.
- **Elbows**: Tucked close to the body to protect the ribs and liver.
- **Eyes**: Look through the eyebrows. Chin down, eyes up.

## Southpaw Stance (Left-Handed)

The southpaw stance mirrors the orthodox stance completely:
- Right foot forward, left foot back
- Right hand leads, left hand is the power hand
- Same width, depth, and weight distribution principles

### Strategic Considerations
- Southpaws have a natural advantage against orthodox fighters because the angles are unfamiliar.
- The lead foot positioning battle is critical in orthodox vs southpaw matchups -- whoever gets their lead foot outside the opponent's lead foot controls the angle.

## Guard Variations

### High Guard (Peek-a-Boo)
- Both fists are held high, close to the forehead, with elbows tight against the body.
- Provides excellent protection against head punches.
- Requires good body movement to compensate for limited vision.
- Made famous by Mike Tyson and trainer Cus D'Amato.

### Philly Shell (Shoulder Roll)
- The lead hand drops to waist level; the lead shoulder rises to protect the chin.
- The rear hand stays pressed to the cheekbone.
- Punches are deflected by rolling the lead shoulder.
- Requires excellent timing and reflexes. Not recommended for beginners.

### Classic Guard
- Both hands at chin level, slightly extended.
- Good balance between offense and defense.
- The most commonly taught guard for beginners.
- Allows quick transition to any punch or defensive movement.

### Cross-Arm Guard
- Forearms crossed in front of the face, elbows close together.
- Provides a wall of defense against straight punches.
- Limited offensive transition speed.
- Useful when under heavy fire and needing to survive.

## Weight Distribution Adjustments

| Situation | Distribution | Reason |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| Neutral / waiting | 50/50 | Balanced for any action |
| Preparing to attack | 55% rear | Loaded for explosive forward movement |
| Preparing to counter | 60% rear | Ready to shift weight into counter punch |
| Moving forward | Weight shifts forward naturally | Momentum assists the combination |
| Moving backward | Weight shifts rearward | Maintains balance during retreat |
| Throwing the cross | Shifts from rear to centre | Weight transfer generates power |
| Throwing the hook | Pivots through centre | Rotational force needs a central axis |

## Common Stance Mistakes

1. **Feet too wide**: Limits mobility and slows footwork.
2. **Feet too narrow**: Reduces balance and power base.
3. **Flat-footed**: Stay on the balls of the feet for quick movement.
4. **Chin up**: Exposes the chin to uppercuts and straight punches.
5. **Hands too low**: Opens the head to hooks and jabs.
6. **Elbows flared out**: Exposes the ribs and liver.
7. **Squared up (facing opponent directly)**: Presents a larger target area.
8. **Weight too far forward**: Vulnerable to counter punches and off-balance when pushed.

## Drills for Stance and Guard

1. **Stance Check Drill**: Set a timer for 3 minutes. Hold your fighting stance. Every 15 seconds, a partner pushes you lightly from different angles. If you stumble, your stance needs work.
2. **Mirror Work**: Spend 5 minutes per session checking stance in a mirror. Verify foot width, hand position, chin tuck, and shoulder alignment.
3. **Shadow Box with Focus on Return**: After every punch combination, consciously return to a perfect guard. Make the return to guard a reflex.
4. **Tennis Ball Guard Drill**: Tuck a tennis ball under your chin. If it falls during shadow boxing, your chin is coming up.
5. **Footwork Ladder**: Use an agility ladder while maintaining proper stance. Move forward, backward, and laterally without breaking form.
